# PROJECT_VISION.md
## Career Intelligence Platform

**Status:** Pre-development / Founding Vision
**Audience:** Founding team, early engineers, design partners, early investors
**Purpose:** Establish a shared, durable understanding of *why* this product exists before a single line of code is written.

---

## 1. Problem Statement

Careers are the highest-stakes, longest-duration decisions most people make, and they are made with almost no real intelligence.

A person will spend 80,000+ hours of their life working. They will make career decisions — which job to take, which skill to learn, which company to trust, when to leave, when to negotiate, when to pivot — dozens of times over a lifetime. And yet the information available to support those decisions is shallow, manipulated, or simply absent.

Today, career decisions are made using:

- **Anecdote** ("my friend said this company is good")
- **Vanity signals** (follower counts, endorsement counts, logos on a resume)
- **Unverified self-reporting** (job titles, "skills," company reviews written by anonymous, unverifiable parties)
- **Survivorship bias** (we hear from people who succeeded, rarely from the much larger group who didn't)
- **Black-box algorithms** optimized for platform engagement, not for the user's actual career outcome

There is no system today that treats a career the way a serious financial decision is treated — with real data, real risk modeling, real outcome tracking, and a long time horizon. People research a $2,000 laptop purchase more rigorously than they research a job that will shape the next five years of their life.

The Career Intelligence Platform exists to close this gap: to bring the same rigor to career decisions that exists in domains like finance, health, and law — fields where decision-support tools evolved specifically because the stakes were too high for guesswork.

---

## 2. Why Current Solutions Are Insufficient

It is important to be precise about this, because the failure of incumbents is not that they are poorly built — it is that they are **structurally misaligned** with the user's actual interest.

### LinkedIn
LinkedIn's business model is built on engagement, advertising, and recruiter subscriptions — not on the user's career outcome. This produces predictable distortions:
- The newsfeed rewards performative content over honest signal.
- "Endorsements" and "recommendations" are social favors, not validated competence data.
- Job recommendations are influenced by who is paying to be seen, not by genuine fit.
- There is no mechanism for tracking whether a connection, a course, or a job change actually *helped* a user's career — LinkedIn has no concept of long-term outcome.

LinkedIn is, at its core, a **visibility marketplace**, not a decision-support system.

### Handshake
Handshake solves a narrower problem — connecting students to early-career job postings — but suffers from related issues:
- It is optimized for *placement volume*, not placement quality or long-term fit.
- It has limited ability to track what happens after the application: Did the person get the job? Did they stay? Did it advance their trajectory?
- Its data ends at the point of hire, which is precisely where the most important career questions begin.

### Glassdoor / Indeed / Job Boards in General
- Reviews are anonymous, unverified, and vulnerable to manipulation (by both disgruntled ex-employees and company-incentivized positive reviews).
- Aggregated ratings collapse highly contextual experiences (role, team, manager, level) into a single misleading star rating.
- Job boards are search interfaces over postings, not interpreters of what those postings mean for a specific person's trajectory.

### Career Coaches, Resume Services, and Influencers
- Advice scales by personality and confidence, not by evidence.
- There is no feedback loop — a coach who gives bad advice faces no consequence, because outcomes are never measured against the advice given.
- Access is gated by price, making good guidance a privilege rather than a default.

### The Common Thread
Every existing player is optimized for a transaction (a click, a subscription, a job application, a placement) rather than for a **trajectory**. None of them ask, track, or answer the question: *"Did this decision actually make this person's career better, by their own definition of better?"*

This is the gap the Career Intelligence Platform is built to fill.

## 3. Target Users

We are designing for several interconnected user groups. Their needs differ, but they share a common reliance on the same underlying intelligence layer.

### Primary: The Career Decision-Maker
Individuals at any career stage — early career, mid-career pivot, senior leadership transition — who are facing a consequential decision: which job to take, whether to stay, what skill to invest in, when to negotiate, when to leave a field entirely. This is our core user and the one whose trust we must earn first.

### Secondary: The Career-Curious
People not actively job-seeking, but who want an ongoing, ambient understanding of their market position, skill relevance, and trajectory health — analogous to how someone might passively monitor their credit score or investment portfolio without actively trading.

### Tertiary: Institutions Seeking Honest Signal
- **Employers** who want access to validated, outcome-linked signal about candidates (not just self-reported resumes), and who benefit from a marketplace with less noise and more truth.
- **Universities and career centers** who need to understand, in aggregate and with evidence, which programs and pathways actually produce strong outcomes for their students.
- **Researchers and policymakers** who currently lack reliable longitudinal data on labor market mobility and need an evidence base for workforce policy.

### Explicit Note on Sequencing
We will resist the temptation to build for all of these simultaneously. The platform's credibility is built bottom-up: if individual users do not trust the intelligence layer, no institutional user will either. Institutional trust is earned, not sold.

## 4. Long-Term Vision

**In ten years, we want the Career Intelligence Platform to be the system people instinctively consult before making any consequential career decision — the way a person checks a map before a long drive, or a financial dashboard before a large purchase.**

We envision a world where:

- A person can see, with real evidence, what tends to happen to people *like them* after they make a given career choice — not as a guarantee, but as a calibrated, probabilistic picture.
- Career advice is held to the same evidentiary standard as medical or financial advice: claims are traceable, outcomes are tracked, and bad advice loses credibility over time rather than being amplified by confidence alone.
- Every person — regardless of network, pedigree, or financial means — has access to the kind of personalized, evidence-based career intelligence that today is only available to those who can afford an elite coach or who happen to know the right people.
- The platform becomes a trusted longitudinal record of a person's *own* career — not a performative profile, but a private, honest instrument panel they can return to for years.
- Labor markets, in aggregate, become slightly more efficient and slightly more humane, because better-informed individuals make better-matched decisions, and that compounds across millions of people over time.

We do not see ourselves as a job board, a networking app, or a recruiting tool. We see ourselves as **decision-support infrastructure for the career layer of people's lives** — closer in spirit to a navigation system or a financial planning tool than to a social network.

## 5. Core Philosophy

Several beliefs are foundational to this product and should act as a filter for every major decision the team makes:

### 5.1 Outcomes over engagement
We do not win by maximizing time-on-platform. We win when a user makes a better decision and then *needs us less* for a while, because their situation has stabilized. A product that creates dependency through anxiety is a failure, even if it is profitable.

### 5.2 Evidence over confidence
Career advice is currently dominated by whoever sounds most confident. We will treat confidence and accuracy as separate variables, and we will always make the underlying evidence — its strength, its sample size, its limitations — visible to the user, not just the conclusion.

### 5.3 The individual is the client, not the product
In a marketplace where employers, universities, and platforms all have an incentive to influence the user, the platform itself must have a clear, stated loyalty: to the individual whose career is on the line. Any feature that quietly trades user trust for institutional revenue is a violation of the core contract.

### 5.4 Humility about prediction
Careers are non-stationary, deeply personal, and affected by factors no model fully captures (timing, relationships, luck, values that resist quantification). We will present our intelligence as a *decision aid that improves the odds*, never as a deterministic forecast. Overclaiming certainty is a near-term growth tactic and a long-term trust killer.

### 5.5 Long time horizons are a feature, not a bug
Most products in this space are built for a single transaction (the application, the placement). We are explicitly building for the multi-year, multi-decision arc of a career. This means our value sometimes shows up months or years after a given recommendation — and our metrics and patience must reflect that.

### 5.6 Privacy and dignity by default
A tool that knows this much about someone's professional anxieties, failures, and ambitions carries an unusually high duty of care. Users must always control who sees their data, and the platform must never use vulnerable moments (layoffs, rejections, burnout) as growth or monetization opportunities.

## 6. Success Metrics

We deliberately define success in a way that resists vanity metrics. Metrics are grouped by what they actually tell us.

### Trust & Honesty Metrics (north star candidates)
- **Calibration accuracy**: When the platform expresses confidence in an outcome (e.g., "70% of similar profiles see a salary increase within 18 months of this move"), how closely do realized outcomes match stated probabilities over time?
- **User-reported decision confidence**, measured before and after using the platform for a specific decision.
- **Independent trust score**: a measured willingness of users to rely on the platform for a high-stakes decision (not just a casual one), tracked over time via direct survey and behavioral proxies.

### Outcome Metrics
- **Longitudinal trajectory improvement**: tracked, consented changes in compensation, role seniority, skill relevance, and self-reported satisfaction among users who acted on platform guidance versus comparable users who did not.
- **Decision regret rate**: the proportion of users who, in retrospective follow-up, say they would make the same decision again.
- **Time-to-clarity**: how quickly a user moves from uncertainty to a confident decision when using the platform, compared to self-reported baseline.

### Health Metrics (guardrails, not growth levers)
- **Healthy disengagement rate**: the proportion of users who resolve their immediate need and naturally reduce usage, without churning due to dissatisfaction. We will treat *appropriate* disengagement as a positive signal, distinct from churn.
- **Anxiety-inducing pattern flags**: internal monitoring for features that correlate with increased user-reported stress or compulsive checking behavior, with an explicit mandate to redesign or remove such features.

### Business Sustainability Metrics
- Revenue and growth metrics exist and matter, but are treated as **lagging indicators of trust**, not goals in themselves. Any growth metric that improves while trust or outcome metrics decline is treated as a red flag requiring investigation, not a win to celebrate.

## 7. Guiding Principles

These are operating rules intended to be referenced during real product and design debates.

1. **If a feature only works because the user doesn't fully understand it, it is not allowed.** Transparency about how recommendations are generated is non-negotiable, even when opacity would convert better.
2. **No dark patterns, ever — including "soft" ones.** No fake urgency, no manufactured scarcity, no guilt-based retention prompts.
3. **Every claim of the form "people like you tend to..." must be traceable to an explainable basis.** If we cannot explain *why* a recommendation was made in plain language, we do not ship it.
4. **The product must be honest about its own limitations.** Where data is thin, biased, or unrepresentative for a given user's situation, the platform should say so rather than presenting false confidence.
5. **Negative or discouraging information must still be delivered when it is true and useful.** We will not optimize for the user "feeling good in the moment" at the expense of the user being well-informed.
6. **Institutional customers (employers, universities) never get to see more about an individual than that individual has explicitly chosen to share.** The intelligence layer serves the individual first; institutional products are built on top of that consent, never around it.
7. **We design for the user's worst day, not just their best day.** Features should be stress-tested against scenarios like layoffs, rejection, burnout, and career stagnation — moments when people are most vulnerable and most easily exploited.
8. **Slow and trusted beats fast and viral.** Growth tactics that trade away long-term credibility for short-term acquisition are off-limits, even when competitors use them.
9. **Every team member can trace any feature back to a user outcome it is meant to improve.** "It increases engagement" is not, by itself, an adequate justification for any feature.

## 8. Non-Goals

Explicitly defining what we are *not* building is as important as defining what we are. This list exists to prevent scope drift and mission dilution.

- **We are not a social network.** We are not optimizing for posting, broadcasting, or social validation loops. Any social features that emerge must serve the decision-support mission directly, not general engagement.
- **We are not a job board.** Surfacing job postings may be a feature, but it is not the core product, and we will not measure ourselves primarily on application or placement volume.
- **We are not an advertising business.** We will not sell influence over recommendations to employers, course providers, or any third party. Monetization must never compromise the integrity of the intelligence layer.
- **We are not a credentialing authority.** We do not aim to replace degrees, certifications, or formal credentials; we aim to help users interpret and contextualize them.
- **We are not building a general-purpose AI career chatbot as the core value proposition.** Conversational interfaces may be part of the experience, but the durable value is the underlying evidence and tracking infrastructure, not a chat window.
- **We are not optimizing for maximum daily active usage.** A user who checks in quarterly and makes excellent decisions is a greater success than a user who checks in daily out of anxiety.
- **We are not, at this stage, building tools for layoffs, performance management, or other employer-side HR functions.** Building anything that could be perceived as primarily serving employer interests over individual interests in our early years would undermine the trust the entire platform depends on.

## 9. Product Roadmap

This roadmap is organized by **maturity phase**, not fixed calendar dates, since the priority is sequencing the right capabilities and proof points rather than committing to deadlines prematurely.

### Phase 0 — Foundation of Trust (Pre-Launch / Early Access)
Goal: Prove that honest, evidence-based career intelligence is possible and valuable at small scale, before any growth push.
- Establish the core data philosophy: what counts as a verified outcome, how consent and privacy work, how uncertainty is represented.
- Work with a small, high-trust cohort of early users across a few career stages to validate that the core intelligence is genuinely useful, not just novel.
- Build the foundational measurement infrastructure for calibration and outcome-tracking from day one — not bolted on later.

### Phase 1 — Individual Decision Support (Early Product)
Goal: Become genuinely useful for a single, well-defined high-stakes decision moment.
- Focus deeply on one or two decision types (e.g., evaluating a job offer, deciding whether to leave a role) rather than spreading thin across every possible career question.
- Establish the feedback loop: track what users decided and what happened afterward, with their consent.
- Begin publishing internal calibration results to hold ourselves accountable, even before this is user-facing.

### Phase 2 — Longitudinal Intelligence (Growth Phase)
Goal: Expand from single-decision support to an ongoing, ambient understanding of a user's trajectory.
- Introduce the concept of a private, longitudinal career record the user owns and controls.
- Expand the range of decisions supported (skill investment, lateral moves, geographic moves, career pivots).
- Begin cautious, consent-driven aggregation of anonymized outcome data to strengthen the evidence base.

### Phase 3 — Ecosystem Trust Layer (Maturity Phase)
Goal: Extend trusted intelligence outward to institutions, without compromising individual-first loyalty.
- Offer opt-in, consent-based products for employers and universities that rely on the same honesty standards established for individuals.
- Explore credential-adjacent verification tools that help good outcomes become legible to the broader market, always controlled by the individual.
- Begin contributing anonymized, aggregate research insights back to the broader labor market (academic partners, policymakers) as a public-good function of the platform's scale.

### Ongoing, Cross-Phase Commitments
- Calibration auditing and trust metrics are never deprioritized in favor of feature velocity.
- Every phase transition requires a explicit re-affirmation that non-goals (Section 8) still hold.

## 10. Future Expansion Ideas

These are deliberately speculative and not commitments. They exist to stretch our thinking about the platform's ultimate ceiling, and should be revisited periodically rather than treated as a backlog.

- **Career "stress testing"**: scenario modeling that lets a user explore "what if" branches (a recession, an industry downturn, an AI-driven shift in their field) against their own profile, similar to financial stress-testing tools.
- **Skill half-life intelligence**: tracking and surfacing how quickly specific skills are losing or gaining market relevance, so users can make investment decisions about their own learning with the same rigor as a financial portfolio decision.
- **Cross-generational mobility insights**: anonymized, aggregate research into how career mobility is changing across generations, potentially valuable as a public-interest contribution and thought-leadership platform.
- **Employer honesty scoring**: an evidence-based, outcome-linked counterpart to today's unverifiable review sites — grounded in tracked, consented outcomes rather than anonymous anecdotes.
- **Career "second opinions"**: a structured way for users to get an independent, evidence-grounded perspective on a major decision they've already informally made elsewhere (e.g., with a coach, mentor, or friend), as a check against confirmation bias.
- **Policy and research partnerships**: becoming a recognized, responsibly-governed data partner for labor economists and policymakers seeking better visibility into real-world career mobility patterns.
- **Global and cross-market expansion**: adapting the intelligence layer to different labor markets, education systems, and cultural norms around career change, rather than assuming a single (e.g., U.S.-centric) model of career progression.
- **Life-decision adjacency**: cautious, far-future exploration of whether the same evidence-based decision-support philosophy could extend to deeply adjacent life decisions (e.g., further education, relocation) — approached carefully, and only if it does not dilute the core career mission.

## Closing Note

This document is a living artifact. It should be revisited at every major milestone, not as a formality, but as a genuine check: *are we still building the thing we set out to build, or have we drifted toward the easier, more familiar incentives of the industry we set out to replace?*

The single test for any future decision this team makes should be simple to state, even if hard to honor:

**Would we be proud to show this decision to a user who trusted us with one of the most important choices of their life?**
